Physics-Guided Operational Stiffness Proxies for Reduced-Order Screening of Harmonic Resonance Risk in 33/11 kV Distribution Substations
1. Background

Harmonic resonance in medium-voltage distribution networks arises from interactions between network inductance, shunt capacitance, and harmonic current sources associated with nonlinear loads and power electronic converters. When the network impedance exhibits resonance at frequencies close to dominant harmonic emissions, voltage distortion can be significantly amplified. Such amplification can increase equipment stress, accelerate insulation degradation, and undermine power quality compliance.

In 33/11 kV distribution substations, resonance conditions are influenced by several factors including feeder loading patterns, capacitor bank operation, network configuration, and variations in system strength. Accurate identification of resonance-prone conditions is therefore an important aspect of power quality management in modern distribution networks.

Traditional harmonic resonance analysis methods rely on frequency-domain impedance modelling, harmonic measurement campaigns, or electromagnetic transient simulations. While technically rigorous, these approaches require detailed network models and specialized monitoring infrastructure that are rarely available across large populations of distribution substations.

Consequently, many utilities lack scalable analytical tools capable of identifying potential resonance vulnerability using only routinely available operational measurements.

2. Knowledge Gap

Despite extensive research on harmonic resonance analysis, three limitations remain evident in the literature:

Model dependency

Most resonance assessment methods require detailed network impedance models or harmonic injection data, which are rarely available in operational datasets.

Monitoring dependency

Many studies rely on waveform-level harmonic measurements, limiting their applicability to substations equipped with advanced power quality monitoring systems.

Limited operational analytics

Few studies explicitly investigate how routine operational variables such as voltage levels, feeder loading patterns, and load dynamics can be used to infer resonance susceptibility.

As a result, a methodological gap exists between detailed harmonic resonance analysis and the practical need for scalable screening tools that can operate using routine substation data.

3. Research Aim

The aim of this research is to develop a physics-guided reduced-order screening framework capable of identifying harmonic resonance susceptibility in 33/11 kV distribution substations using routine operational measurements.

The framework is designed to translate operational measurements into interpretable indicators that approximate electrical conditions associated with resonance amplification.

4. Research Question

How can routinely available operational measurements be transformed into physics-guided indicators capable of screening harmonic resonance susceptibility in 33/11 kV distribution substations under sparse monitoring conditions?

5. Research Objectives

To develop physics-guided proxy indicators that represent electrical conditions associated with resonance susceptibility in distribution substations.

To reconstruct representative operating regimes of substations using clustering of operational proxy variables.

To formulate a reduced-order resonance susceptibility model based on operational approximations of network stiffness and loading stress.

To construct an interpretable probabilistic risk screening model capable of estimating resonance-prone operating conditions.

To evaluate the robustness and practical applicability of the proposed framework through validation and sensitivity analysis.

Electrical stiffness in power systems is typically characterized by short-circuit strength or Thevenin impedance. However, such quantities are not directly observable in routine operational datasets.

To address this limitation, this study introduces operational stiffness proxies derived from voltage–load sensitivity relationships.

7. Methodology

The proposed framework consists of five analytical stages.

7.1 Operational Data Reconstruction

Routine substation operational data will be collected, including busbar voltages, transformer loading, feeder currents, and feeder power flows. Data preprocessing will include timestamp synchronization, outlier detection using median absolute deviation filtering, missing value interpolation, and normalization relative to equipment ratings.

7.2 Physics-Guided Proxy Indicator Construction

Operational measurements will be transformed into proxy indicators representing electrical conditions associated with resonance susceptibility.

These indicators include:

• voltage depression index
• voltage imbalance index
• incomer utilization ratio
• feeder utilization ratio
• feeder loading concentration index
• load ramp rate
• feeder ramp dispersion
• operational network stiffness proxy

These indicators collectively capture voltage margin conditions, loading stress, load concentration behaviour, dynamic operational changes, and electrical stiffness.

7.3 Operating-State Reconstruction

Substation operating conditions vary over time due to changes in demand patterns and network configuration.

To capture these variations, proxy indicator datasets will be clustered using the Partitioning Around Medoids (PAM) algorithm. Cluster medoids represent typical substation operating regimes and enable regime-dependent analysis of resonance susceptibility.

7.5 Probabilistic Risk Screening

A probabilistic screening model will be developed using cumulative logit ordinal regression.

The model estimates the probability that a given operating condition belongs to ordered resonance risk categories such as low, moderate, or high risk. This approach preserves interpretability while enabling probabilistic characterization of resonance vulnerability.

8. Validation and Robustness Analysis

The framework will be evaluated through multiple validation procedures.

Time-series cross-validation will assess model generalization across different operational periods. Sensitivity analysis will examine the influence of proxy indicators on risk predictions. Robustness tests will evaluate the stability of results under variations in clustering parameters and risk thresholds.

Benchmark comparisons will be conducted against simpler screening methods based solely on voltage thresholds or loading conditions.

9. Methodological Novelty

The proposed framework introduces three methodological innovations:

Operational stiffness proxies

A data-driven approximation of network electrical stiffness derived from voltage–load sensitivity relationships.

Reduced-order resonance susceptibility modelling

A physically interpretable function linking operational proxies to resonance vulnerability without requiring harmonic measurements.

State-conditioned resonance risk screening

A regime-aware risk assessment framework that accounts for variations in substation operating conditions.

10. Scientific Contributions

This research contributes to power quality analysis in distribution networks by:

• proposing a reduced-order methodology for screening harmonic resonance susceptibility using operational data
• introducing physics-guided proxy indicators linking routine measurements to resonance-related electrical mechanisms
• developing an operational stiffness approximation framework for resonance vulnerability assessment
• integrating operating-state reconstruction with probabilistic risk modelling for practical substation analytics

11. Practical Significance

The proposed framework provides utilities with a scalable screening tool capable of identifying potentially vulnerable substations without requiring extensive harmonic monitoring infrastructure or detailed network impedance models. By enabling early identification of resonance-prone operating conditions, the approach supports targeted monitoring deployment, preventive mitigation strategies, and improved power quality management in distribution networks.

1. Research Design

This study adopts a physics-guided computational research design to estimate harmonic resonance susceptibility in 33/11 kV distribution substations using routine operational measurements.

Rather than directly measuring harmonic distortion, the proposed framework approximates resonance amplification conditions through operational proxies representing electrical characteristics associated with resonance vulnerability.

The framework integrates physical reasoning with data-driven analysis to construct a reduced-order screening model capable of identifying operating conditions with elevated resonance susceptibility.

The analytical pipeline consists of five stages:

Operational data reconstruction → physics-guided proxy construction → operating regime reconstruction → reduced-order resonance susceptibility modelling → probabilistic risk screening

2. Substation Operational Data Reconstruction
2.1 Data Sources

The analysis utilizes routine SCADA measurements from a 33/11 kV distribution substation. Typical operational variables include:

• busbar voltage magnitude
• incomer current
• transformer loading
• feeder currents
• feeder real power flows

These measurements are typically recorded at:

• hourly resolution, or
• 15-minute resolution.

The observation horizon spans approximately 6–12 months to capture seasonal, weekly, and daily variations in substation operating conditions.

2.2 Data Pre-Processing

Operational datasets are prepared through the following steps.

Timestamp Alignment

All feeder and incomer measurements are synchronized to a common time index.

Missing Value Treatment

Missing values are handled using either

• Kalman smoothing, or
• seasonal interpolation.

Outlier Detection

Outliers are identified using a Median Absolute Deviation (MAD) filter.

Per-Unit Normalisation

Electrical variables are normalized relative to equipment ratings to allow comparison across feeders.

Final Method Statement

This study proposes a physics-guided reduced-order framework for screening harmonic resonance susceptibility in 33/11 kV distribution substations using routine operational data. Operational measurements are transformed into proxy indicators representing voltage margin, loading stress, feeder concentration, dynamic behaviour, and electrical stiffness. Substation operating regimes are reconstructed using k-medoids clustering. A reduced-order resonance susceptibility model derived from harmonic amplification principles is then used to estimate vulnerability conditions. Finally, probabilistic resonance risk screening is performed using cumulative logit ordinal regression, with robustness analysis and benchmark comparisons used to evaluate the framework.
